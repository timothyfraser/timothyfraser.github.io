## Teaching Statement

As an educator, I rely on **active learning** to help students learn political science, data science, and methods. The active learning I employ is intense focus on student-led practice, as opposed to a lecture format — I dedicate two-thirds of class time to workshops, labs, and team projects, reserving the remaining third for lessons, discussion, and weekly guest scholars.

I have taught methods to masters and undergraduate students, across political science and interdisciplinary contexts. Since 2022 I've taught advanced statistics and data science at Cornell for the Systems Engineering Program — masters students' core statistics coursework, in R. This has included **Systems Engineering & Six Sigma** (2022–2025), **Systems Architecture** (2023–2024), **Data Science for Socio-Technical Systems** (Summer 2024), and **MEng Research Projects** (2023–2025). Previously I taught three undergraduate courses at Northeastern: Quantitative Techniques for Political Science (Spring 2020, Fall 2021) and Research Methods (Summer 2021), plus TA work on Comparative Politics, American Politics, and a Japan study-abroad program.

In course evaluations, students rate my teaching **4.65 / 5 on average** (s.d. 0.16), above department and university means on teaching effectiveness, use of class time, feedback quality, inclusivity, availability, and enthusiasm.

## Active Learning through Workshops

Once a week, students conduct a workshop. Together in small groups, they learn and apply new techniques based on the lesson content — mapping local businesses, learning new visualizations in R, role-playing a social network to learn how social capital affects voting.

> *"He was really patient with us as we all learned how to code... I appreciate how much he cared about our learning and the efforts he made to make sure we understood and learned to apply our material."*

## Discovery-based Learning through Labs

Later in the week, students apply their learning in labs to a contemporary social science question. My methods students deployed survey experiments to test marketability of a new Dunkin' Donuts flavor. Other labs use difference-of-means to test the Fukushima disaster's effect on Japanese renewable adoption. Some labs end with short reports emulating methods/results/discussion structure of research papers, so students practice articulating value-added, limitations, and future directions.

## Inclusivity through Projects

For term projects, I commission groups to answer a real policy question with empirical data (past groups: crime rates, terrorism, emissions, partisanship). The same groups stay together all term, building trust through peer reviews, short presentations, and honor codes. Discussion themes include racial discrimination, sexism, mental health, and different ways of learning. I've invited 21 weekly guest speakers (75% women, 33% people of color) so students see expertise reflected in the field.

## Research with Students

At Northeastern I ran three capstones for 10 public-policy masters students, training student teams to record data and analyze results. Together we produced publishable papers on Louisiana recovery after Katrina, Sandy disaster-recovery committees in New York, and Boston community spaces. I also led an undergraduate working group of 8 students, producing 8 peer-reviewed studies. At Cornell I'm running a growing roster of MEng student teams on dashboards, APIs, ML emissions surrogates, and global bikeshare networks.
