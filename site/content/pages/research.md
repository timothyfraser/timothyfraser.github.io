## Research Summary

I am a computational social scientist and political methodologist applying data science and mixed methods to help communities combat and adapt to climate change. Trained as a political scientist in mixed methods, my research examines why some cities, counties, and states produce better environmental-policy outcomes than others — emissions, transportation mobility, health, and crisis resilience. I focus especially on local-level policies and community resources: **social infrastructure** and the social ties that enable trust, reciprocity, and collective action.

Since 2017 I've published **50+ peer-reviewed studies** on adaptation to crisis in US and Japanese cities — in *Global Environmental Change*, *Environmental Innovations and Societal Transitions*, *Energy Research & Social Science*, *Scientific Reports*, *Environmental Politics*, and *npj Clean Air*, among others. My dissertation measured how social capital shapes urban evacuation, disaster recovery, and energy transition.

I've collaborated with **18+ scholars**, reviewed for **30+ journals**, and consulted for the **UN Development Programme**'s Accelerator Labs in Mexico and Paraguay. In October 2024, I advised OECD researchers in Paris on urban analytics for social-infrastructure mapping. Past work has been funded by local and national grants — a 2016 Fulbright fellowship, Natural Hazards Center grants, and 2020 Fulbright Hays and Japan Foundation doctoral fellowships (declined due to COVID-19). I currently coordinate the **Center for Transportation, Environment, and Community Health (CTECH)** — a multi-million-dollar US-DOT-funded center across four universities.

## Methodology: Networks, Mapping, & Measurement

I've built a diverse methodological toolkit testing how structure and representation in social and political networks shape urban policy outcomes — and vice versa. The recurring tools are **network statistics**, **GIS**, and **quasi-experiments**, applied to emissions, hurricane evacuation, disaster recovery, pandemic outcomes, and mobility — often using cell-phone mobility data and committee-level network data.

- **Recovery from Japan's 2011 Triple Disaster** — social network analysis of 600+ members across 39 committees; cities with more interconnected committees and more community-center investment saw better economic recovery.
- **Hurricane Sandy recovery committees** in New York, working with masters capstone students.
- **Tokyo ward emissions & environmental committees** — ongoing.
- **Mapping evacuation with big data** — Facebook mobility data across 10 fires, floods, hurricanes, and earthquakes; later linked to social capital, government trust, and pandemic spread.
- **Networks of solar cities and companies** in Japan — mapping adoption against community resources.

## Mapping Social Infrastructure

With Daniel Aldrich and Courtney Page-Tan (Aldrich Resilience Lab, Northeastern), we developed methods to measure **social infrastructure** — the community spaces that build social capital — using publicly available geospatial data and the Google Places API. Pilots ran in Fukuoka, then Boston; with Cornell undergraduates we've now produced social-infrastructure estimates for the **25 largest US cities**. With Takahiro Yabe (MIT) and Daniel Aldrich, we're using anonymized mobility data to ask **who** uses these sites — which types of social infrastructure bring together residents across social strata, building the bridging ties that support civic life.

## Dashboards, Cloud Computing, & AI

At Cornell I lead software development for the **Climate Action in Transportation (CAT)** system with Dr. Oliver Gao. CAT lets local decision-makers estimate national, state, and county-level air pollution emissions from transportation through a user-friendly online dashboard — built atop EPA's MOVES, which is otherwise hard for non-experts to run. The deliverables include the **VISUALIZER** ShinyApp suite, the **catr** R package for programmatic MOVES, **moveslite** + **calculator** for fast cached estimates (seconds instead of minutes), and a growing AI/optimization layer for automated reporting and scenario selection.

## Polarization & Health

Drafted and administered two national surveys with colleagues on trust, polarization, and well-being. Findings: cross-party division is linked to worse health; close in-group bonding ties buffer that effect; polarization also predicts COVID-19 vaccination uptake.
